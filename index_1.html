Inference & Testing

Central Limit Theorem, hypothesis testing, p-values, confidence intervals and Bayesian posteriors — the backbone of statistical reasoning.

A/B Calculator
Z-test or Welch's t-test for comparing two group means.
z = (x̄B − x̄A) / √(σ²A/nA + σ²B/nB)
Distribution Preview
Sampling distributions of both groups.

Under H₀: the two distributions are identical. The test statistic measures how many standard errors apart the observed means are.

p-value: probability of observing a test statistic this extreme, assuming the null is true. If p < α, we reject H₀.

Beta-Binomial Posterior
Bayesian inference for conversion rates using a Beta prior.

Posterior mean: (α₀ + k)/(α₀ + β₀ + n) — the best estimate of the true conversion rate.

P(B>A): estimated via Monte Carlo sampling from both posteriors.

§ 02

Z-Scores, CI & Effect Size

Standardisation, confidence intervals, and effect size measures — the tools that connect raw data to actionable interpretation.

Z-Score Transformer
Standardise any observation to see where it falls in the standard normal distribution.
Z = (X − μ) / σ

Standardisation: Z tells you how many standard deviations X is above or below the mean. Z=0 is the mean; Z=±1 covers ~68% of data; Z=±2 covers ~95%.

Percentile: Φ(Z) — the proportion of values below X. If Z=1.64 then ~95% of values lie below this point.

Standard Normal
Shaded area = percentile at the current Z-score.
CI Calculator
Confidence interval for a population mean. Choose known σ (Z-interval) or estimated s (t-interval).
CI = x̄ ± z*·(σ/√n)  or  x̄ ± t*·(s/√n)

Interpretation: A 95% CI means: if we repeated the experiment many times, ~95% of the computed intervals would contain the true parameter.

Margin of Error: Half-width of the CI. Decreases as n grows (∝ 1/√n) or as confidence level drops.

Interval Visualisation
The interval and how it changes with n.
Effect Size — Cohen's d & h
Practical significance: how big is the difference in standardised units? Complements the p-value.
d = (μ₂ − μ₁) / σ_pooled
h = 2·arcsin(√p₂) − 2·arcsin(√p₁)

Convention (Cohen, 1988): d < 0.2 = negligible · 0.2–0.5 = small · 0.5–0.8 = medium · >0.8 = large.

Why it matters: Statistical significance (p-value) depends on sample size. Effect size is independent of n — it measures the magnitude of the difference itself.

Distribution Overlap
Visualise how Cohen's d maps to distributional overlap.
t-distribution vs Normal
The t-distribution has heavier tails when df is small. It converges to N(0,1) as df → ∞.
f(t, ν) ∝ (1 + t²/ν)^(−(ν+1)/2)
t → N(0,1) as ν → ∞

When to use t: When σ is unknown and estimated from sample (practically always). For n > 30, the difference from Z is negligible.

Critical value: The value t* such that P(|T| > t*) = α. Larger than z* because of heavier tails, especially at low df.

R²: proportion of variance in Y explained by the model. R²=1 is perfect fit; R²=0 means the model explains nothing.

RMSE: root mean squared error — on the same scale as Y, interpretable as the typical prediction error.

MAE: mean absolute error — robust to outliers unlike RMSE.

Residual variance: variance remaining after the model accounts for the trend. Low residual variance = model captures most of the signal.
